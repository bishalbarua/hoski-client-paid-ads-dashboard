## Section 1: The Five Laws — Service Business Translations

The Five Laws hold. The execution beneath each law changes significantly depending on whether your client sells physical products online or sells a service that requires a phone call, a consultation, or an appointment. This section adds the service business translation for each law.

---

### Law 1 Translation: Know the Math Before You Spend the Money

**The original breakeven model:**
```
(AOV × margin) = max CPA
```

This works for e-commerce. It does not work for a dental practice, a law firm, a med spa, or a home renovation company. Use the following models instead.

---

**Service Business Breakeven Model:**

```
(Average Revenue per New Client)
  × (Lead-to-Client Conversion Rate)
  × (Show Rate)
= Max Cost Per Lead
```

**Step by step:**

1. Get the average revenue per new client from the client. For a dental implant practice like Voit, this is approximately $29,900. For a med spa like FaBesthetics, it depends on the service mix — get a weighted average.
2. Get the lead-to-close rate. What percentage of people who book a consultation actually become paying clients? If Voit closes 30% of consultations, and 70% of booked appointments actually show, the math is:

```
$29,900 (avg revenue) × 30% (close rate) × 70% (show rate) = $6,279 expected value per lead

If the client's gross margin on that procedure is 60%, then:
$6,279 × 60% = $3,767 gross profit per lead

A max CPL at a 3:1 return on ad spend = $1,256
A target CPL at 70% of that = $879
```

3. Set your target CPL at 70-80% of the max CPL. Same principle as DTC. Different inputs.

**High-ticket / consultation-first note:**

For clients with average contract or case values above $5,000 (Park Road Furniture at $5k-$15k+, GDM custom jewelry, Voit Dental at $29,900, legal cases), the CPL ceiling is naturally higher. A $200 CPL that closes into a $15,000 job is a 75:1 return. Do not apply DTC-calibrated CPL anxiety to these accounts.

**What to ask the client in Week 1:**

- What is your average revenue per new client or patient?
- What percentage of people who book a consultation actually show up?
- Of the people who have a consultation, what percentage convert to a paying client?
- What is your approximate gross margin on that service?

If the client does not know these numbers, that is the first problem to solve. Do not run ads until you have a defensible CPL target.

### Law 2 Translation: Creative Is the Variable

The original law is correct. For DTC, creative volume is the lever. For service businesses, creative volume matters less. Message precision and trust-building matter more.

**Adjusted expectations by client type:**

| Client Type | Creative Volume Target | Test Cycle Length | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| DTC e-commerce | 4-5 per cycle | 72 hours | High purchase volume, fast data |
| Med spa / aesthetics | 2-3 per cycle | 5-7 days | Longer consideration, lower volume |
| Dental (general) | 2-3 per cycle | 5-7 days | Appointment-based, lower daily conversions |
| Dental (high-ticket implants) | 1-2 per cycle | 10-14 days | $29,900 decision, very low daily conversion volume |
| Chiropractic / functional med | 2-3 per cycle | 5-7 days | Condition-specific targeting, slower data |
| Legal | 1-2 per cycle | 10-14 days | Case intake is low-volume |
| Construction / home services | 2-3 per cycle | 7-10 days | Seasonal, low-volume, long project cycle |
| High-ticket furniture / jewelry | 1-2 per cycle | 10-14 days | Long consideration cycle, low conversion volume |

**Creative angles that work for service businesses:**

| Angle | When to Use | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Provider credentials + trust | Medical, legal, dental | Doctor's background, years of experience, specific training |
| Before / after outcome | Dental, aesthetics, chiropractic | Patient results with specific outcomes (compliantly framed) |
| "You're not alone" empathy | Chronic pain, mental health, family medicine | Normalize the problem, show understanding before solution |
| Social proof / volume | Any service with reviews | "500+ 5-star reviews" / "8,000 consultations completed" |
| Cost comparison | High-ticket (Voit vs. ClearChoice) | "Our patients save $20,000+ vs. national chains" |
| Process demystification | Any consultation-gated service | "Here's exactly what happens at your first appointment" |
| Financing availability | Dental implants, custom furniture, high-ticket med | "As low as $X/month" — reduces sticker shock |
| Founder / practitioner on camera | Med spa, boutique dental, aesthetics | The provider IS the brand in single-provider businesses |

**The whitelisting play applies equally to service businesses.** A practitioner running their own content from their personal handle — not a business account — performs the same way a founder handle does in DTC. For FaBesthetics (Becky) or Texas FHC (Dr. B), this is high leverage. Use it.

### Law 3 Translation: Trust the Blended Number, Not the Platform

**MER does not apply to service businesses.**

MER requires a single total revenue figure from a unified commerce platform (Shopify, etc.) divided by total ad spend. Most service clients cannot produce that number cleanly. A dental practice has insurance billing cycles, multi-visit treatment plans, and cash-pay procedures tracked across different systems. A law firm's revenue is case settlements that may close months after the initial ad click.

**The service business equivalent: Cost Per Qualified Outcome**

Define "qualified outcome" specifically for each client. Then track it blended across all channels — not per platform.

| Client | Qualified Outcome | How to Track |
|---|---|---|
| Voit Dental | Completed consultation that results in a treatment plan | GHL pipeline stage — "Treatment Plan Presented" |
| Texas FHC | Confirmed new patient appointment that shows | CallRail + GHL — "Appointment Attended" |
| FaBesthetics | Booked consultation | GHL pipeline — "Consult Booked" |
| Anand Desai Law | Qualified intake call (3+ minutes, relevant case type) | CallRail — duration filter + case type tag |
| New Norseman | Qualified quote request (project in budget range) | GHL pipeline — "Qualified Lead" stage |
| Park Road Furniture | Showroom visit booking or design consultation request | GHL pipeline or form submission |

**The service business dashboard (replaces the DTC dashboard):**

| Metric | Source | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Target CPL | Unit economics calc (above) | The ceiling |
| Actual CPL | Platform + CallRail | Are we hitting target? |
| Show Rate | GHL pipeline | Are leads showing up? |
| Lead-to-Close Rate | GHL pipeline | Is the funnel working post-lead? |
| Cost Per Qualified Outcome | Combined platforms / GHL | Is the business growing from ads? |
| Call Duration Distribution | CallRail | Are calls the right length to be qualified? |
| Channel Split | Google / Meta / LSA | Which platform drives qualified outcomes? |

If platform CPL looks healthy but show rate is below 50%, you have a lead quality problem. If show rate is healthy but close rate is low, you have a sales or offer problem — not an ads problem. Know the difference and communicate it clearly to the client.

### Law 4 Translation: The Calendar Is the Strategy

The annual calendar principle holds. The events change entirely for service businesses.

**Service Business Tier 1 Events by Vertical:**

**Dental:**
- January: New insurance year — patients with unused benefits are highly motivated. This is Tier 1.
- Pre-summer (April/May): Cosmetic and elective procedures peak before summer social season.
- September: Back-to-school / end-of-year insurance utilization awareness.
- BFCM: Only applicable if running a specific offer on elective cosmetic services.

**Medical / Chiropractic / Functional Medicine:**
- January: New Year health resolutions. New insurance coverage begins. Highest intent period for elective health decisions.
- September: Back-to-school / return to routine.
- November: Pre-deductible-reset urgency push (patients want to use remaining insurance benefits before year-end).

**Med Spa / Aesthetics:**
- Pre-summer (April/May): Body treatment season.
- Pre-holiday (October/November): Facial, skin, and appearance services peak.
- Valentine's Day: Gift cards and treatment packages. Tier 1 for aesthetics.
- Mother's Day: Tier 1 for aesthetics.

**Legal:**
- No universal Tier 1. Map to the client's case types. Personal injury practices have seasonal peaks tied to weather (winter accidents, summer travel). Family law peaks around January (post-holiday separation decisions) and September (back-to-school transitions).

**Construction / Home Services:**
- March-April: Spring season start. Homeowners plan summer projects. Highest intent.
- August-September: Fall renovation window before winter.
- January: Planning season — budget has reset, projects are being scoped.

**High-ticket retail (jewelry, furniture):**
- Valentine's Day: Tier 1 for jewelry.
- Mother's Day: Tier 1 for jewelry and home decor.
- Pre-holiday (November): Gift and home refresh season.
- BFCM: Applicable if a promotional offer makes sense for the brand.

**Apply the same 4-week prep rule** from the original framework to all of the above. The process does not change. The dates do.

### Law 5 Translation: The Funnel Is the Product

The funnel architecture changes for service businesses because the conversion event is not a purchase — it is an action that triggers a human follow-up (call, consultation, appointment).

**Service Business Funnel Architecture:**

```
TOP OF FUNNEL (Awareness + Intent Capture)
Goal: Get qualified prospects to raise their hand
Campaigns: Google Search (high-intent terms), Meta awareness
Budget: 50-60% of total spend

MIDDLE (Lead Nurture + Conversion)
Goal: Convert expressed interest into a booked appointment
Channels: Retargeting ads + GHL automated sequences (SMS/email)
Budget: 25-30% of total spend

BOTTOM (Show + Close)
Goal: Ensure booked leads show up and convert
Channels: GHL reminders, re-engagement sequences, review requests
Budget: 10-15% of total spend (GHL operational, not ad spend)
```

**The GHL pipeline is part of the funnel.** For service business clients, what happens after the form submission or phone call is as important as what happens before it. An ad campaign that generates 50 leads with a 40% show rate and 25% close rate is worse than one that generates 30 leads with a 75% show rate and 45% close rate. Monitor the full pipeline, not just the lead volume.

**The landing page rule for service businesses:**

Every landing page must have:
- Clear statement of the primary service and who it is for (above the fold)
- One primary CTA — for most service businesses this is "Book a Consultation" or "Call Now," not "Learn More"
- Provider credentials and trust signals within first scroll (years of experience, licenses, certifications, press)
- Social proof specific to the outcome (not just star ratings — specific results, specific patient or client stories)
- Objection handling: address cost, process, and "is this right for me?" before they have to ask
- Mobile-first (70%+ of traffic is mobile — this is the same as DTC)
- For medical / legal: compliance-reviewed copy (see Section 6)

**Retargeting windows for service businesses:**

The original framework recommends 7-14 day retargeting windows. That is correct for DTC impulse purchases. For service businesses with longer consideration cycles, extend:

| Vertical | Retargeting Window |
|---|---|
| General dental / medical / aesthetics | 30 days |
| High-ticket dental (implants) | 60-90 days |
| Legal | 60 days |
| Construction / home renovation | 90 days |
| High-ticket furniture / jewelry | 60-90 days |

**Budget split for service businesses:**

Replace the default 70% Meta / 25% Google / 5% testing split with the following starting points, adjusted per client:

| Client Type | Google | Meta | LSA | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Dental (general) | 40% | 30% | 30% | LSA is high-leverage for dental |
| Dental (high-ticket) | 50% | 40% | 10% | Search intent is primary; Meta for retargeting |
| Medical / chiropractic | 50% | 40% | 10% | Search for condition terms, Meta for awareness |
| Med spa / aesthetics | 30% | 60% | 10% | Visual product, Meta-dominant |
| Legal | 70% | 20% | 10% | Search intent is almost everything |
| Construction / home reno | 60% | 30% | 10% | Search-heavy, local targeting |
| High-ticket retail | 40% | 50% | 0% | Meta visual for discovery, Google for branded |
| B2B (Hoski.ca) | 40% | 30% | 0% | LinkedIn 30% if applicable |

## Section 2: Law 6 — Lead Quality Is the Metric (Service Businesses)

*This law applies to every service business client. It does not apply to DTC e-commerce.*

---

A lead is not a conversion. A cheap lead that never shows is worse than an expensive lead that converts. For service businesses, the job is not to generate leads. The job is to generate qualified pipeline.

**Define "qualified" before you launch.**

Before the first campaign goes live, answer this question with the client: *What makes a lead worth our time?* Document the answer in the client info file.

Examples:
- Voit Dental: Qualified = person in the geographic market with interest in full arch implants, not already a current patient, willing to come in for a free consultation.
- Texas FHC: Qualified = person experiencing chronic pain or a specific condition the practice treats, not a current patient, willing to book an initial appointment.
- Anand Desai Law: Qualified = person with an active legal matter in a practice area Anand handles, calling (not just submitting a form), call duration above 3 minutes.
- New Norseman: Qualified = homeowner in the GTA with a renovation project in the $50,000+ range.

Write this down. Revisit it with the client monthly. If the client's definition of qualified is shifting, the campaigns need to shift with it.

---

**The four numbers that replace MER for service businesses:**

| Metric | What It Tells You |
|---|---|
| Cost Per Lead (CPL) | Platform efficiency. The input. |
| Show Rate | Lead quality. Did they show up? If below 60%, something is wrong upstream (targeting, ad copy, landing page, or follow-up speed). |
| Lead-to-Close Rate | Sales and offer effectiveness. If this is low, the problem is not the ads. |
| Cost Per Acquired Client | The real number. CPL / (show rate × close rate). This is what you optimize toward. |

If you can only track one thing beyond CPL, track show rate. It is the earliest signal of lead quality and the first place to diagnose problems before they show up in the client's revenue.

---

**Speed-to-lead is a campaign health metric.**

The research is consistent: the probability of qualifying a lead drops by 80% if the response time exceeds 5 minutes. For service business clients, how fast the practice follows up with a new lead is directly tied to the campaign's effective conversion rate. If show rate is low, check follow-up speed in GHL before adjusting the campaign.

Recommended follow-up protocol for all service business clients:
- Immediate automated SMS + email from GHL within 60 seconds of form submission
- Human follow-up attempt within 5 minutes during business hours
- 3-attempt sequence over 48 hours if no response
- If no contact after 48 hours, move to long-term nurture sequence (not dead lead)

This is not a campaign optimization task. It is an operational task. When lead quality is the problem, this conversation with the client is your highest-leverage intervention.

---

**Feeding lead quality back to the algorithm.**

Most service business campaigns optimize on "lead form submitted" or "thank you page visited." This trains the algorithm to find people who fill out forms — not people who show up and buy. For clients with sufficient volume, implement offline conversion imports:

1. Define a downstream conversion event (appointment attended, consultation completed)
2. Export that data from GHL weekly
3. Import it into Google Ads and Meta as an offline conversion event
4. Shift campaign optimization to that event instead of the initial form fill

This improves lead quality over time because the algorithm learns what a real customer looks like, not just what a form-filler looks like. It requires coordination with the client and a minimum volume threshold (approximately 30+ conversion events per month per platform to be statistically useful).

## Section 4: Compliance Reference — Healthcare and Legal Clients

*This section applies to: Dentiste, Voit Dental, FaBesthetics, Texas FHC, Synergy Spine, Serenity Familycare, Bloomer Health, Som K. Plastic Surgery, Anand Desai Law Firm.*

*Non-compliance does not just risk ad disapproval — it risks account suspension and client liability. Read this before touching any healthcare or legal account.*

---

### Meta Advertising Restrictions for Healthcare

**Special Ad Categories:**
Meta classifies health and wellness ads under its Special Ad Category policies. Certain health-related targeting is restricted regardless of whether you formally select a special ad category.

**What you cannot target:**
- Health conditions, diagnoses, or symptoms as interest-based targets (you cannot target "back pain" or "diabetes" as an interest)
- Lookalike audiences built from health-related engagement data (e.g., a pixel event tied to a "chronic pain" page on the client's site)
- Behavioral segments that imply health status

**What this means in practice:**
- Use broad targeting or location-based targeting for healthcare clients on Meta
- Do not build custom audiences from health-condition-specific landing pages and use them for lookalikes
- Interest-based targeting is limited to general demographics, life events, or broadly adjacent categories

**Ad copy restrictions:**
- Cannot imply a diagnosis: "Do you have peripheral neuropathy?" is potentially non-compliant. "Are you experiencing chronic foot or leg pain?" is safer.
- Cannot guarantee outcomes: "We will fix your back pain" is non-compliant. "Many of our patients report significant relief after treatment" is compliant.
- Cannot use before/after imagery in a misleading way — must accurately represent typical patient outcomes
- For plastic surgery and cosmetic procedures: Meta has specific policies against ads that promote "idealized" body image. Review the current Meta Advertising Policies for health and beauty before running these campaigns.

**When in doubt:** Run the ad copy past the client's office manager or practice coordinator before publishing. They often have compliance training that the ads team does not.

---

### Google Ads Restrictions for Healthcare

**Healthcare advertiser verification:**
Google requires healthcare advertisers in certain categories (prescription drugs, clinical trials, addiction treatment) to complete a verification process. For most Höski healthcare clients (dental, chiropractic, med spa), standard verification applies.

**Ad copy:**
- Google's healthcare ad policies are less restrictive than Meta on targeting but equally restrictive on claims
- No guaranteed outcomes in copy ("cure," "eliminate," "fix your X permanently" are all high-risk)
- No claims that a treatment is "FDA-approved" unless you can verify this and it is accurate

**Local Service Ads (LSA) for healthcare:**
- Google LSA for healthcare requires the provider to complete a background check and license verification
- This is the client's responsibility, not the agency's — but flag it in Week 1 if LSA is part of the plan
- LSA for healthcare typically shows for high-intent searches like "dentist near me" or "chiropractor [city]" and converts at a higher rate than standard search at a lower effective CPL

---

### Legal Advertising Restrictions

**State bar rules apply to legal advertising.** These vary by state. The agency is not responsible for legal compliance, but account managers should be aware:

- Most state bars prohibit: direct claims of being "the best" or "most experienced" without substantiation
- Most state bars require: disclaimers on attorney advertising (often a small "Attorney Advertisement" label)
- Some states restrict: direct solicitation language for personal injury or criminal defense

**Practical guidance:** All ad copy for legal clients should be reviewed by the client before it goes live. Get email confirmation. This protects the agency.

**For Anand Desai Law specifically:** Confirm which state bar rules apply. Document the disclaimer requirement if any.

**Creative angles that work:**
- Cost comparison to competitors ("Save $20,000+ vs. ClearChoice")

### Legal (Anand Desai Law Firm)

**What this business is actually selling:** Representation for a specific legal matter. The prospect has an immediate need — they are not browsing.

**Primary platforms:** Google Search (75%), Meta (15%), LSA (10%)

**What makes this vertical unique:**
- Purchase intent is entirely search-driven. No one scrolls Instagram looking for a lawyer.
- Practice area segmentation is non-negotiable. One campaign per practice area. Do not mix.
- Call quality is the only real conversion signal. A form submission from a legal prospect is worth very little — they need to talk to someone.
- CallRail is the primary measurement tool, not pixel events.
- Response speed is a competitive differentiator. Legal inquiries go to the first firm that responds.

**Highest-leverage campaigns:**
- Google Search, segmented by practice area ("immigration lawyer Montreal," "personal injury lawyer [city]")
- LSA if available and eligible in the client's practice areas
- Meta is secondary at best — consider only for remarketing or brand awareness, not lead generation

**Creative angles that work:**
- Case outcome reference (where permissible by state bar rules)
- Experience and credentials: years in practice, cases handled, specific expertise
- Initial consultation offer: "Free case review"
- Trust and availability: "Available 7 days a week," "Responsive and personal service"

**Tracking setup:**
- CallRail as primary conversion tracking
- Call duration threshold: set a qualified call minimum (typically 2-3 minutes)
- Manual call review weekly — pull 10 recent calls and review for qualification
- Do not optimize campaigns on raw call volume. Optimize on qualified calls.

**KPI targets:** CPL varies widely by practice area. Get a benchmark from the client's current intake data first. Cost per qualified call typically $80-200 for competitive legal markets.

**Compliance note:** Review ad copy with client before launch. Document approval by email. (See Section 4.)

### Construction and Home Services (New Norseman)

**What this business is actually selling:** Custom home builds and high-ticket renovations. Project values are $50,000-$500,000+. The lead-to-close cycle is 3-12 months. The conversion event is a qualified quote request.

**Primary platforms:** Google Search (60%), Meta (30%), LSA (10%)

**What makes this vertical unique:**
- Strong seasonality. The acquisition window is concentrated (spring and early fall). Off-season budget should shift to brand-building and retargeting.
- Project scope filtering is critical. "Home renovation" attracts both a homeowner with a $500K project and one with a $5K bathroom re-caulk job. Use qualifying language in ad copy and the intake form to filter.
- Instagram is genuinely high-leverage for portfolio content. Visual proof of craftsmanship builds trust and enables retargeting of engaged users.

**Highest-leverage campaigns:**
- Google Search on project-specific and intent-specific terms ("custom home builder Toronto," "home renovation contractor GTA," "kitchen renovation [city]")
- Meta: Instagram-first visual creative showing portfolio work. Retarget video viewers and post engagers.
- Google Display retargeting for website visitors

**Creative angles that work:**
- Portfolio quality — high-production before/after photos or video walkthroughs
- Founder/owner credibility (Kris Potts visible in content)
- Process transparency: "Here's how a Norseman renovation works, from first call to final walkthrough"
- Qualifying language in copy: "For homeowners planning a renovation over $50,000"
- Proof of local presence and community (GTA-specific references)

**Intake form guidance:** Add one qualifying question to the contact form: "What is your approximate project budget?" with ranges. This single field dramatically improves lead quality by filtering low-budget inquiries before they reach the sales pipeline.

**KPI targets:** CPL $60-150, cost per qualified lead $150-400, cost per proposal $500-1,500

### High-Ticket Retail (Estate Jewelry, Global Diamond Montreal, Park Road Furniture)

**What makes this category different from standard DTC:**

These clients have physical products but sell in a fundamentally different way than a typical DTC brand:
- GDM and Park Road are consultation-first, in-person closing businesses
- EJPR is online-only but with one-of-a-kind inventory and a 35-year brand
- Average order values are $1,000-$15,000+
- Purchase consideration cycles are weeks to months
- Creative volume is limited by catalog constraints (one-of-a-kind pieces cannot be featured forever once sold)

**EJPR-specific notes:**
- 511 active products is actually an advantage for dynamic creative and DPA retargeting
- The "40-70% below retail" positioning is the primary message and should be the lead in every ad
- Media credentials (History Channel, Anderson Cooper, The View, WSJ) are extremely high leverage as trust signals and are underused
- Google Shopping is high-leverage given the product catalog volume

**GDM-specific notes:**
- The "manufacturer, not retailer" positioning is the core differentiator — lean into it
- Bilingual (EN/FR) campaigns require separate ad sets with language-specific copy, not just translated ads
- Custom ring consultation is the high-value conversion event — the purchase comes second
- Affirm financing integration (in progress as of last update) should be front-and-center in ad copy once live

**Park Road-specific notes:**
- AOV of $5k-$15k means almost no one buys without a showroom visit
- The conversion event is an in-store consultation booking, not an online purchase
- Creative should focus on the showroom experience and craftsmanship, not the product in isolation
- Retargeting window of 90 days minimum — these decisions take time

### B2B (Hoski.ca)

*Hoski markets itself, which means the agency has a direct feedback loop. What works here can be validated and documented.*

**Primary platforms:** Google Search (40%), Meta (30%), LinkedIn (30% — if budget allows)

**What this business is actually selling:** Trust that you can grow someone's business. This is a high-consideration B2B purchase. The decision-maker is a business owner, not a marketing manager.

**Conversion event:** Strategy call booking

**Creative angles that work:**
- Case studies with specific numbers ("We helped [client type] grow from $X to $Y in [timeframe]")
- Outcome-first positioning ("Turn your website into a 24/7 sales machine")
- Targeting pain, not features: business owners care about customers and revenue, not "conversion-optimized ad copy"
- Video testimonials from client-types the prospect identifies with

**LinkedIn guidance:** If budget allows, LinkedIn is high-leverage for Hoski.ca because it allows targeting by company size, industry, and job title (business owner, founder, CEO in the $1M-$10M revenue range). It is expensive on a CPL basis but the lead quality is higher than Meta for B2B.
